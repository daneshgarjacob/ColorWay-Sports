The NFL just released its updated Global Markets Program for 2026, and it's the biggest version yet. Twenty-nine of the league's 32 teams are now assigned to international markets across 22 countries. The idea is simple: give teams marketing rights in specific countries so they can build fanbases overseas, sell merchandise, and eventually play games there.

Italy is the newest market, with the Cleveland Browns and New Orleans Saints getting the rights. The Rams are also heading to Melbourne for the first-ever NFL game in Australia.

## Our Take

We get what the NFL is trying to do here, and on paper it makes sense. Assign teams to countries, let them market themselves, build local fanbases, sell jerseys, and eventually play games there. It's a structured way to grow the sport internationally.

But we're not sure this is how fandom actually works.

Think about it this way. In basketball, the Lakers are one of the most popular teams on the planet. Nobody assigned them to a country. LeBron James, Kobe Bryant, Shaq, Magic Johnson built that brand by winning and being larger than life. The Yankees are the most recognizable baseball team in the world. Same thing. The Dodgers are everywhere. It happened organically through decades of winning and star power.

Does assigning the Chargers to Greece mean people in Athens are going to start buying powder blue jerseys? Maybe. But probably not because of a marketing program. It would happen because Justin Herbert makes a highlight that goes viral, or because the Chargers play a game there and someone falls in love with the sport.

The teams that make sense in this program are the ones that are already global brands. The Chiefs make sense with 7 countries because they just won three Super Bowls and have Patrick Mahomes. The Rams in Asia makes sense because they're in Los Angeles, the entertainment capital of the world. The Dolphins in Latin America makes sense because of Miami's connection to that region.

Where it gets questionable is the smaller assignments. The Saints in France? Sure, New Orleans has French roots, but are people in Paris going to become Saints fans because of a marketing program? The Browns in Nigeria? We love the ambition, but we're not sure that's how this works.

We think the NFL would be better off pushing the league as a whole internationally and letting the big brands do the heavy lifting. The Super Bowl is already a global event. The NFL doesn't need to assign the Commanders to the UAE for people there to discover football. They just need to keep making the product entertaining, play more international games, and let star power do what it's always done.

That said, the program is growing every year, and 29 of 32 teams are now involved. The only teams sitting out are the Bills, Bengals, and Titans. Whether or not this specific approach moves the needle, the NFL's commitment to going global is real, and it's only going to accelerate from here.
